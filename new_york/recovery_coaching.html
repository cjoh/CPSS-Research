Legal Status Overview

Independent Practice Permitted

State-Defined Term: Certified Addiction Recovery Coach (CARC), Certified Recovery Peer Advocate (CRPA)

Regulatory Status: Voluntary certification (not statutory licensure)

Independent Practice: Not explicitly prohibited; must maintain strict non-clinical boundaries

Legal Definition

New York defines a recovery coach as:

"Anyone wishing to promote recovery from addiction by connecting members with recovery support services designed to build recovery gains, create individual recovery options and help to remove barriers to recovery."

Key Characteristic: Recovery coaches operate at a NON-CLINICAL LEVEL.

Coaching vs. Therapy Boundaries

Recovery Coaching (Permitted)

  • Non-clinical support services
  • Recovery support and connection to services
  • Peer-based support using lived experience
  • Recovery navigation and advocacy
  • NO diagnosis, medication, or clinical services

Mental Health Counseling (Requires License)

NY Education Law Article 163 § 8402 defines mental health counseling as including:

  • Evaluation and assessment
  • Treatment and amelioration
  • Using assessment instruments
  • Mental health counseling and psychotherapy

Critical: Only licensed persons may practice mental health counseling or use the title "mental health counselor"

Critical Warning

Any coach who delivers services that mirror the scope of practice of a licensed psychotherapist risks FELONY charges.

Violation of scope constitutes unauthorized practice and can result in potential felony charges.

Independent Practice Requirements

Certification Types

  • Certified Addiction Recovery Coach (CARC)
  • Certified Recovery Peer Advocate (CRPA)
  • Certified Recovery Peer Advocate Provisional (CRPA-P)

Training Requirements

  • Total of 46 hours of training through ASAP-NYCB-approved courses
  • CCAR Recovery Coach Academy (30 hours)
  • Peer Ethics (16 hours)

Independent Practice Status

Can recovery coaches operate independently? YES - No explicit statutory prohibition found

Recovery coaches can operate independently BUT must stay strictly within non-clinical boundaries. Any activity resembling psychotherapy, clinical assessment, diagnosis, or treatment planning could constitute unauthorized practice of mental health counseling.

Scope of Practice

What Recovery Coaches CAN Do

  • Provide non-clinical support services
  • Connect members with recovery support services
  • Build recovery gains through peer support
  • Create individual recovery options
  • Help remove barriers to recovery
  • Share lived experience
  • Provide recovery navigation

What Recovery Coaches CANNOT Do

  • Diagnose mental health or substance use conditions
  • Assess or treat mental health conditions
  • Provide psychotherapy or clinical counseling
  • Develop clinical treatment plans
  • Prescribe or recommend medication
  • Use assessment instruments for clinical purposes

Important Considerations

Risk Management Recommendations

  • Maintain clear documentation distinguishing non-clinical services
  • Have explicit service agreements clarifying non-clinical nature of work
  • Obtain professional liability insurance
  • Establish clear referral relationships with licensed professionals
  • Stay current on scope of practice boundaries through continuing education
  • Never cross the line into activities requiring licensure

Key Legal Distinction

Mental health counselors: MUST be licensed under Education Law

Recovery coaches: NOT licensed profession, voluntary certification only

The boundary is legally enforced: Crossing the line risks felony unauthorized practice charges
