Recovery Coaching: Ethical Practice Guide

Navigating the Gray Line Between Coaching and Therapy

Executive Summary

Who This Guide Is For

Recovery coaches who work with individuals who are:

  • Out of active rehab/treatment
  • In recovery (not actively using)
  • Need support integrating recovery tools into daily life
  • Require accountability and peer support
  • Benefit from someone with lived experience

The Critical Distinction

✓ Recovery Coaching

  • Peer support based on lived experience
  • Accountability and goal-setting
  • Resource connection and navigation
  • Sharing recovery journey
  • Life integration support
  • Recovery planning
  • Motivational support
  • Post-treatment reintegration

Clinical Therapy/Counseling

  • Diagnosis of disorders
  • Treatment of mental health conditions
  • Treatment of substance use disorders
  • Psychotherapy and clinical interventions
  • Clinical treatment planning
  • Medication management
  • Crisis intervention
  • Requires professional license

Universal Ethical Requirements

Required Regardless of State:

1. Ensure Client Has Therapy Support

  • Client must be working with (or have access to) a licensed therapist or counselor
  • Recovery coaching complements therapy; it does not replace it
  • For clients with substance use disorder history, ongoing clinical support is essential

2. Verify Client Is In Recovery

  • Client should be out of acute treatment phase
  • Should have established sobriety/recovery
  • Should have clinical support system in place
  • Active users in crisis need clinical intervention, not coaching

3. Make Outbound Referrals

  • Maintain referral network of licensed professionals
  • Refer immediately for suicidal ideation, relapse requiring medical intervention, severe mental health crisis
  • Document referrals appropriately

4. Clear Scope Definition

  • Use coaching language, not clinical language
  • Clearly state you are not a therapist
  • Written agreements defining scope of support
  • Transparency about your lived experience and training

Marketing Language Framework

✓ SAFE Recovery Coaching Language

  • "Supporting your recovery journey"
  • "Peer support from someone with lived experience"
  • "Accountability and goal-setting for sustainable recovery"
  • "Helping you integrate recovery tools into daily life"
  • "Recovery planning and resource connection"
  • "Post-rehab life integration support"
  • "Navigating challenges in early recovery"
  • "Building a recovery-centered life"

✗ AVOID - Crosses Into Therapy

  • "Treating addiction"
  • "Treating mental health conditions"
  • "Diagnosing substance use disorders"
  • "Therapy for addiction"
  • "Counseling services"
  • "Clinical treatment"
  • "Curing" or "fixing" your addiction
  • "Mental health therapy"

Client Eligibility Guidelines

✓ Who You CAN Work With

  • Individuals who have completed inpatient/outpatient treatment
  • People in stable recovery
  • Clients who have established relationship with therapist/psychiatrist
  • Individuals seeking peer support and accountability
  • Those needing help integrating 12-step or other recovery principles
  • Post-rehab clients needing reintegration support
  • People with active clinical care team
  • Clients working on recovery-related life goals

✗ Who You CANNOT Work With

  • Individuals actively using substances without medical supervision
  • People in acute mental health crisis
  • Those needing clinical diagnosis or treatment
  • Anyone who refuses to engage with licensed clinical support
  • Individuals who should be in inpatient treatment
  • Those requiring detoxification
  • Clients with untreated mental health emergencies
  • Anyone requiring clinical assessment or treatment planning

Core Ethical Principles

Essential Practices for All Recovery Coaches:

  • Clinical Support Requirement: All clients must have concurrent licensed clinical support
  • Stay in Your Lane: Never diagnose, treat, or provide clinical interventions
  • Referral Network: Maintain relationships with licensed therapists, counselors, and crisis services
  • Written Agreements: Provide clear contracts defining scope of coaching support
  • Professional Boundaries: Avoid dual relationships and maintain appropriate boundaries
  • Confidentiality: Understand and maintain appropriate privacy standards (coaching, not HIPAA)
  • Lived Experience: Share your recovery journey appropriately without making it about you
  • Professional Liability Insurance: Obtain coverage appropriate for recovery coaching
  • Continuing Education: Stay current on recovery models, ethics, and best practices
  • Cultural Competency: Respect diversity in recovery paths and cultural backgrounds

Navigating the Gray Line

When to Refer vs. When to Coach:

Refer to Licensed Professional When Client:

  • Expresses suicidal ideation or self-harm thoughts
  • Experiences active substance use relapse requiring medical attention
  • Shows signs of severe mental health crisis
  • Needs clinical diagnosis or treatment modification
  • Requires medication management or prescription changes
  • Presents with trauma requiring clinical processing
  • Has symptoms beyond your scope of competence

You Can Coach When Client:

  • Needs accountability for recovery commitments
  • Wants help integrating recovery tools into daily life
  • Seeks peer support from someone with lived experience
  • Needs guidance navigating recovery community resources
  • Benefits from goal-setting for recovery-related life areas
  • Wants support maintaining boundaries in relationships
  • Needs help with post-treatment reintegration challenges

State-by-State Requirements

Regulation Varies by State:

Some states have specific certification requirements for recovery coaches, while others do not regulate the profession. Common certifications include:

  • Highly Regulated States: May require specific certification for recovery peer support (e.g., Connecticut, Florida, Georgia, New York, Pennsylvania)
  • Moderately Regulated States: Offer voluntary certification programs (e.g., Arizona, California, Colorado, Illinois)
  • Unregulated States: No specific requirements, but ethical practice still essential

See the state directory for specific requirements in your state.

Getting Started as a Recovery Coach

Recommended Path:

  1. Establish your own stable recovery (typically 2+ years recommended)
  2. Check your state's specific requirements and certifications
  3. Complete recovery coach training (40-100+ hours depending on certification)
  4. Pursue relevant certification (CRSS, CPRS, CPS, or state-specific)
  5. Obtain professional liability insurance
  6. Create clear coaching agreements and scope documents
  7. Build referral network with licensed clinical professionals
  8. Develop ethical marketing materials using appropriate language
  9. Join professional associations and commit to continuing education
  10. Consider specialized training (trauma-informed care, motivational interviewing, etc.)
