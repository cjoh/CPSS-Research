STATUS: PROHIBITED - Independent Practice Not Allowed

Critical Legal Restriction

Independent Practice: EXPLICITLY PROHIBITED

Employment Requirement: MUST be an EMPLOYEE of a licensed substance use disorder or mental health treatment agency

Cannot: Work as independent contractor or operate private practice

Legal Status Overview

Status: PROHIBITED for independent practice

State-Defined Term: Behavioral Health Coach (licensed)

Created By: Senate Bill 26 (2024)

License Required: Yes - through Division of Professional Licensing (DOPL)

Legal Definition

Utah created the "behavioral health coach" license through Senate Bill 26 in 2024. This license explicitly restricts how recovery coaches may practice.

Behavioral Health Coach (Licensed)

A person licensed by the Division of Professional Licensing who:

  • MUST be employed by a substance use disorder treatment agency or mental health treatment agency
  • Works under general supervision of a licensed mental health therapist
  • Provides care navigation, referrals, assessments, and collaborative planning
  • CANNOT engage in mental health therapy practice

Employment Requirement

MANDATORY EMPLOYEE STATUS

Utah law explicitly requires behavioral health coaches to be EMPLOYEES.

You CANNOT:

  • Work as an independent contractor
  • Operate a private recovery coaching practice
  • Provide services outside of agency employment
  • Offer fee-for-service recovery coaching independently

You MUST:

  • Be employed by a licensed SUD treatment agency OR mental health treatment agency
  • Work under general supervision of licensed mental health therapist
  • Operate within agency policies and procedures
  • Obtain state license through DOPL

Licensing Requirements

Educational Requirements

  • Education: Related 4-year degree from accredited institution
  • License Application: Through Division of Professional Licensing (DOPL)
  • Background Check: Required
  • Supervision: Work under general supervision of licensed mental health therapist

Scope of Practice

Permitted Activities (Within Employment)

Behavioral health coaches working as employees of licensed agencies may provide:

  • Care Navigation: Guiding individuals through behavioral health systems
  • Referrals: Connecting individuals to appropriate services and resources
  • Assessments: Non-clinical assessments within scope (not diagnostic)
  • Collaborative Planning: Working with individuals on recovery planning
  • Support Services: Peer support and recovery-oriented services
  • Resource Connection: Linking to community resources

Prohibited Activities

  • Mental Health Therapy: Cannot engage in therapy practice
  • Clinical Diagnosis: Cannot diagnose mental health or substance use disorders
  • Independent Practice: Cannot work outside of agency employment
  • Unsupervised Practice: Cannot work without therapist supervision
  • Private Practice: Cannot operate fee-for-service private practice

Supervision Requirements

General Supervision Standard

Supervisor Must Be: Licensed mental health therapist

Supervision Type: General supervision (not intensive clinical supervision, but ongoing oversight)

Purpose: Ensure services remain within scope and quality standards are maintained

Practical Guidance

For Those Seeking to Practice in Utah

You must understand: Independent recovery coaching practice is NOT legal in Utah under current law.

Your Options:

  1. Seek Employment: Apply for positions at licensed SUD or mental health agencies
  2. Obtain License: Complete required education and apply for behavioral health coach license through DOPL
  3. Work Under Supervision: Accept that you will work under therapist supervision as an employee

You CANNOT:

  • Start a private recovery coaching business
  • Work as independent contractor providing recovery coaching
  • Offer recovery coaching services outside of licensed agency employment

Legal Risk Warning

Attempting to practice recovery coaching independently in Utah may result in:

  • Practicing without a license (criminal offense)
  • Regulatory action by DOPL
  • Fines and penalties
  • Potential civil liability
