# Life Coach Legal Research: 9-State Comprehensive Analysis

**Research Date:** November 5, 2025
**States Covered:** Oregon, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Vermont

---

## Executive Summary

**Key Finding:** Life coaching is an unregulated profession in all nine states examined. No state requires professional licensing, certification, or registration to practice as a life coach or executive coach. However, life coaches must be careful not to engage in activities that constitute the practice of licensed mental health professions (psychotherapy, counseling, clinical social work).

**Universal Restrictions Across All States:**
- Cannot diagnose mental health conditions
- Cannot treat mental illness, emotional disorders, or substance abuse
- Cannot use therapeutic terminology that implies mental health treatment
- Cannot represent themselves as licensed therapists, counselors, or psychologists
- May face felony charges for unauthorized practice of psychotherapy

**Business Licensing:** While professional licensing is not required, coaches may need to obtain general business licenses at the city, county, or state level depending on local regulations.

## OREGON

### Legal Definition
**No statutory definition** exists in Oregon law for "life coach" or "executive coach."

### Licensing Requirements
**NONE.** Oregon has no legal requirement for life coaches to obtain certification, licensure, or registration to practice.

**Source:** Oregon does not regulate life coaches under state law. The profession is entirely unregulated by state government.

### Regulations Around Coaching Practice
**No state regulations** govern life coaching practice in Oregon. Anyone can legally call themselves a life coach without formal education, training, or state licensing.

### Legal Boundaries Between Coaches and Clinical Professionals

**Clinical Professionals Regulated:**
- Licensed Professional Counselors (LPCs)
- Licensed Marriage and Family Therapists (LMFTs)
- Licensed Clinical Social Workers (LCSWs)
- Psychologists

**Boundary Principle:** Life coaches must not engage in the practice of professional counseling, psychotherapy, or clinical treatment. This includes:
- Diagnosing mental health conditions
- Treating mental illness
- Providing psychotherapy services
- Using clinical assessment tools requiring licensure

### Restrictions and Prohibitions
Life coaches are **prohibited from:**
1. Representing themselves as licensed mental health professionals
2. Providing services that overlap with licensed counseling or therapy
3. Diagnosing or treating mental health conditions
4. Using protected titles (therapist, counselor, psychologist, etc.)

**Potential Consequences:** If coaching services overlap with those of a medical doctor, psychologist, or psychiatrist, the coach can be cited for practicing these professions without a license.

### Relevant Statutes and Regulations
**No specific statutes** regulate life coaching in Oregon.

**Related Statutes:**
- Oregon Revised Statutes (ORS) Chapter 675 - Regulates Licensed Professional Counselors and Therapists
- Oregon Administrative Rules (OAR) Division 3732 and 3739 - Board of Licensed Professional Counselors and Therapists rules

**Note:** These statutes regulate licensed professionals, not life coaches, but define the scope of practice that coaches must avoid.

### Legislative History
**2020:** ICF Oregon successfully prevented an amendment to Oregon House Bill 2303 that would have required life coaches, parenting coaches, and wellness coaches to be licensed and regulated. The amendment was not added to the bill.

### Professional Guidance
**International Coach Federation (ICF) Oregon:** Recommends voluntary ICF certification for credibility and client trust, though not legally required.

### Additional Notes
- No government regulation or oversight of life coaching
- Voluntary professional certification through organizations like ICF is common for credibility
- Business license may be required depending on local jurisdiction

**Reference:**
- Rhode Island General Laws Title 5, Chapter 5-63.2 - Mental Health Counselors and Marriage and Family Therapists

- 216-RICR-40-05-11 - Rules and Regulations for Licensing Clinical Mental Health Counselors and Marriage and Family Therapists

**Boundary Principle:** Life coaches are not regulated by the Rhode Island Department of Health and must not engage in activities that constitute licensed clinical mental health counseling or therapy.

### Restrictions and Prohibitions
Life coaches are **prohibited from:**
1. Diagnosing mental health conditions
2. Treating mental illness or providing psychotherapy
3. Representing themselves as licensed mental health professionals
4. Using protected titles (LCMHC, LMFT, therapist, counselor)
5. Engaging in clinical assessment or treatment planning

### Relevant Statutes and Regulations
**No specific statutes** regulate life coaching in Rhode Island.

**Related Statutes and Regulations:**
- Rhode Island General Laws Title 5, Chapter 5-63.2 - Mental Health Counselors and Marriage and Family Therapists
- 216-RICR-40-05-11 - Licensing Clinical Mental Health Counselors and Marriage and Family Therapists

**Note:** These laws regulate licensed professionals, not coaches, but define protected scopes of practice.

### Professional Guidance

### Additional Notes

- Coaches should maintain clear boundaries and avoid clinical language in marketing materials

## UTAH

### Legal Definition
**No statutory definition** currently exists in Utah law for "life coach" or "executive coach."

### Licensing Requirements
**NONE - Currently.** There are currently no legal requirements mandating a certification to practice as a life coach in Utah. You can start your life coaching business without a specific certification.

**Legislative History:**
- **2024:** Senate Bill 251 (SB251) proposed establishing a registration process for life coaches through the Division of Professional Licensing (DOPL), but the bill was not enacted. The committee recommended further study for potential consideration in the 2025 General Session.
- **2025:** Utah took a different approach with Senate Bill 48 (SB48).

### Recent Legislative Developments - SB48 (2025)

**Senate Bill 48: "Behavioral Health Amendments"**
- **Signed:** March 26, 2025
- **Effective Date:** May 7, 2025
- **Approach:** Rather than regulating life coaches directly, Utah chose to strengthen protections around licensed mental health practice and increase enforcement capabilities.

**Key Provisions of SB48:**

1. **Expanded Definition of Mental Health Therapy:**
   - Added "conveying an opinion as to the validity of an individual's established diagnosis" to the definition of mental health therapy

2. **Mental Health Professionals Education and Enforcement Fund:**
   - Created new fund to support education and training of licensees and the public
   - Provides resources for investigation and enforcement

3. **Enhanced Enforcement Authority:**
   - Gives DOPL resources to investigate life coaches who may be acting unlawfully
   - Allows proactive investigation of those advertising or providing services within protected scope of practice
   - Enables action before harm occurs

4. **Scope Protection:**
   - Expands the scope of practice for mental health therapists
   - Draws "this fence around mental health and what mental health professionals do at the exclusion of everyone else"
   - Makes it clear that only licensed therapists can provide mental health treatment

**Legislative Intent:**
- Prevent bad actors in the mental health field from pivoting to life coaching when they have faced disciplinary action
- Address concerns about life coaches working alongside licensed therapists to circumvent licensing requirements (e.g., coach working with child while licensed professional only sees parent)

**Source:** S.B. 48 Behavioral Health Amendments, le.utah.gov/~2025/bills/static/SB0048.html

### Regulations Around Coaching Practice

**Current Status:** Life coaching remains unregulated in Utah, but life coaches face increased scrutiny if their services fall within the protected scope of mental health practice.

**Post-SB48 Environment:** DOPL can proactively investigate and take action against life coaches who are advertising or holding themselves out for providing services that are clearly in a protected scope of practice.

### Legal Boundaries Between Coaches and Clinical Professionals

**Clinical Professionals Regulated:**
Utah regulates mental health professionals through the Division of Professional Licensing (DOPL):

- **Licensed Clinical Mental Health Counselors**
- **Licensed Marriage and Family Therapists**
- **Licensed Clinical Social Workers**
- **Licensed Psychologists**
- **Licensed Substance Abuse Counselors**

**Regulatory Authority:** Utah Division of Professional Licensing (DOPL)
**Website:** dopl.utah.gov/behavioral-health/

**New Clarity Under SB48:**
The law makes it clear that only licensed therapists can provide mental health treatment. Life coaches can continue to exist unlicensed, but the new bill allows DOPL to proactively go after those who are advertising or holding themselves out for providing services that are clearly in a protected scope of practice.

### Restrictions and Prohibitions

Life coaches are **prohibited from:**
1. Providing mental health treatment
2. Diagnosing mental health conditions
3. Conveying opinions about the validity of established diagnoses (new under SB48)
4. Developing plans to manage mental health conditions
5. Treating mental illness, trauma, or substance abuse
6. Advertising services that fall within protected scope of mental health practice
7. Using protected titles or representing themselves as licensed mental health professionals

**Enforcement History:**
- Over the last decade, Utah licensing authorities cited 25 people for unauthorized practice in the mental health field, including four life coaches
- Citations and fines have been used, but enforcement was historically limited
- One life coach was fined $1,000 for advertising as a "psychotherapist"
- Another individual (Denim Slade) was cited and fined $250 for advertising "Lifespan Integration Therapy" and treating trauma

**Post-SB48:** Enhanced enforcement capabilities and proactive investigation authority.

### Relevant Statutes and Regulations

**Current Legislation:**
- **Senate Bill 48 (2025):** "Behavioral Health Amendments"
  - Effective: May 7, 2025
  - Reference: le.utah.gov/~2025/bills/static/SB0048.html

**Historical Legislation:**
- **Senate Bill 251 (2024):** "Life Coaching Requirements"
  - Proposed registration system for life coaches
  - Not enacted; recommended for further study
  - Reference: le.utah.gov/~2024/bills/static/SB0251.html

**Administrative Rules:**
- Utah Division of Professional Licensing rules for Behavioral Health professions
- Available at: dopl.utah.gov

### Professional Guidance
**Voluntary Certification:** Coaches are encouraged to pursue professional certification through organizations like ICF to demonstrate professional standards.

**ICF Coverage:** Utah is served by ICF High Country chapter.

### Notable Context
Utah's approach has drawn national attention:
- ProPublica investigation documented cases of therapists who lost licenses turning to life coaching
- Concerns about conversion therapy being provided under guise of life coaching
- National discussion about whether life coaching should be regulated
- Utah chose enforcement-focused approach rather than direct regulation of coaching

**Key Sources:**
- ProPublica: "When Therapists Lose Their Licenses, Some Turn to the Unregulated Life Coaching Industry Instead" (2024)
- ProPublica: "New Utah Law Seeks to Crack Down on Life Coaches Offering Therapy Without a License" (2025)

### Additional Notes
- Utah is unique in having recent, specific legislation addressing the life coaching/mental health boundary
- SB48 represents a novel approach: strengthening mental health practice protections rather than regulating coaching directly
- This is the most active state legislature regarding life coaching regulation among the nine states examined
- Life coaches in Utah should be particularly careful about advertising language and scope of services following SB48's effective date (May 7, 2025)

## CROSS-CUTTING THEMES AND LEGAL PRINCIPLES

### Universal Legal Boundaries

Across all nine states examined, life coaches face consistent restrictions:

**1. Diagnostic Prohibition**
Life coaches cannot diagnose mental health conditions. This authority is reserved exclusively for licensed mental health professionals (psychologists, psychiatrists, licensed counselors, clinical social workers).

**2. Treatment Prohibition**
Life coaches cannot treat:
- Mental illness
- Emotional disorders
- Behavioral problems
- Substance abuse and addiction
- Trauma
- Any condition requiring clinical intervention

**3. Title Protection**
Protected titles that coaches cannot use without proper licensure include:
- Therapist
- Counselor
- Psychotherapist
- Psychologist
- Clinical Social Worker
- Licensed Professional Counselor (LPC)
- Licensed Marriage and Family Therapist (LMFT)
- Licensed Clinical Mental Health Counselor (LCMHC)
- Any state-specific licensed designations

**4. Scope of Practice Boundaries**
Life coaches cannot:
- Provide psychotherapy
- Conduct clinical assessments
- Develop treatment plans for mental health conditions
- Prescribe medication or medical interventions
- Practice within the legally defined scope of licensed mental health professions

### Legal Consequences for Unauthorized Practice

**Criminal Charges:**
- Felony charges possible for unauthorized practice of psychotherapy
- Varies by state and severity

**Administrative Actions:**
- Cease and desist orders
- Fines (documented range: $250 - $1,000+)
- Citations for unauthorized practice
- Injunctions

**Civil Liability:**
- Professional malpractice lawsuits
- Negligence claims
- Misrepresentation claims

**Enforcement Reality:**
- Criminal prosecutions are extremely rare (no documented cases in past decade across research)
- Administrative citations are more common but still infrequent
- Utah has documented the most enforcement actions (25 citations over 10 years, including 4 life coaches)
- Enforcement typically occurs after consumer complaints

### Confidentiality and Legal Protections

**Licensed Therapists:**
- Protected by HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)
- State confidentiality laws apply
- Privileged communications in legal proceedings
- Mandatory reporting requirements for specific situations

**Life Coaches:**
- NOT protected by HIPAA
- NO confidentiality privilege in legal proceedings
- CAN be subpoenaed to testify about client communications
- Should use coaching agreements with confidentiality clauses (but these are contractual, not statutory protections)

### Executive Coaching Distinction

**Finding:** Executive coaching and business coaching are treated identically to life coaching across all nine states:
- No licensing requirements
- No certification mandates
- No state regulation
- Same restrictions apply (cannot diagnose/treat mental health conditions)

**Exception:** If providing advice requiring other professional licenses (securities, insurance, legal advice), appropriate licensure in those fields would be required.

### Licensed Professionals Offering Coaching

**Special Consideration:** Licensed therapists, counselors, psychologists, and social workers who also offer coaching services face unique considerations:

1. **Dual Accountability:** May be held to their professional licensing standards even when providing "coaching" services
2. **Scope Ambiguity:** Licensing boards may view coaching as falling under their regulatory scope
3. **Ethical Requirements:** Must maintain professional ethics and boundaries
4. **Documentation:** Should clearly distinguish coaching from clinical services
5. **Risk:** Higher liability risk due to professional licensure

**Example:** Texas BHEC explicitly warns that licensed professionals are not exempt from discipline simply because services are labeled "coaching."

### Business Licensing Requirements

**Key Distinction:** Professional licensing (not required) vs. business licensing (may be required)

**Business Registration Factors:**
1. **Entity Type:**
   - Sole proprietorship: Often no state registration required
   - LLC/Corporation: State filing required

2. **Location-Based Requirements:**
   - **City-level:** Many cities require business licenses
   - **County-level:** Generally less common
   - **State-level:** Some states require state business licenses
   - **Home-based:** May need home occupation permits

3. **Zoning Compliance:**
   - Residential zoning may restrict business operations
   - HOA restrictions may apply
   - Lease agreements may prohibit business use

**Recommendation:** Coaches should check local city/county requirements as these vary significantly by jurisdiction.

### Marketing and Advertising Considerations

**Risk Areas:**
1. **Clinical Language:** Avoid terms like "treat," "diagnose," "therapy," "counseling," "cure"
2. **Outcome Claims:** Avoid guaranteeing therapeutic outcomes or mental health improvements
3. **Credentials:** Clearly distinguish coaching certifications from clinical licenses
4. **Scope Description:** Clearly describe coaching as distinct from therapy/counseling
5. **Mental Health References:** Avoid language suggesting treatment of mental health conditions

**Safer Approach:**
- Focus on goal achievement, personal development, life transitions
- Use terms like "support," "facilitate," "guide," "empower"
- Clearly state that coaching is not therapy or counseling
- Include disclaimers about scope and limitations

### Insurance Recommendations

While not legally required in any of the nine states, professional liability insurance is strongly recommended:

**Coverage Types:**
1. **Professional Liability Insurance:** Protects against malpractice claims
2. **General Liability Insurance:** Protects against general business risks
3. **Cyber Liability:** For online coaching and data protection

**Rationale:**
- No statutory protections for coaches
- Potential for claims even when practicing within scope
- Legal defense costs even for frivolous claims
- Protection of personal assets

## CONCLUSION

Life coaching remains an unregulated profession across all nine states examined (Oregon, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, and Vermont). No state requires professional licensing, certification, or registration to practice as a life coach or executive coach.

However, this lack of regulation comes with significant responsibilities. Life coaches must carefully maintain boundaries with licensed mental health professions, avoiding diagnostic or therapeutic services that require clinical licensure. The consistent legal principle across all states is that while coaching is unrestricted, any crossing into the scope of practice of licensed mental health professionals can result in serious legal consequences, including criminal charges, administrative penalties, and civil liability.

The trend toward enforcement rather than regulation (as exemplified by Utah's 2025 SB48) suggests that states are more likely to strengthen protections around existing licensed professions rather than create new regulatory frameworks for coaching. This places the burden on individual coaches to understand and respect professional boundaries.

Coaches practicing in any of these nine states should:
1. Clearly distinguish their services from licensed mental health care
2. Avoid diagnostic and therapeutic language and activities
3. Maintain appropriate referral relationships with licensed professionals
4. Consider voluntary professional certification for credibility
5. Obtain proper business licenses at the local level
6. Carry professional liability insurance
7. Stay informed about evolving legal and regulatory landscapes
