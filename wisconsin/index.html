Overview

Wisconsin has promoted professional peer support since 2010 through the Division of Care and Treatment Services. The state offers Certified Peer Specialist (CPS) certification for individuals with lived experience of mental health or substance use disorders who wish to provide peer support services. Training is coordinated by the Wisconsin Peer Specialist Employment Initiative and is provided FREE of charge.

Training is Free!

Wisconsin provides FREE training for Certified Peer Specialists. The only cost is the $60 exam registration fee.

Official Certification Names

  • Certified Peer Specialist (CPS) - For individuals with lived experience
  • Certified Parent Peer Specialist (CPPS) - For parents/guardians of children with lived experience

Eligibility Requirements

For Certified Peer Specialist (CPS)

  1. Age: At least 18 years old
  2. Education: High school diploma or G.E.D. certificate
  3. Lived Experience: Must have own lived experience of mental health and/or substance use challenges
  4. Recovery Status: Must be in recovery (timeframe not specified, but must demonstrate recovery)
  5. Training: Complete approved CPS training course
  6. Examination: Pass the CPS certification exam with 85% or higher

For Certified Parent Peer Specialist

Same requirements as CPS, but focused on parents/guardians of children with mental health or substance use challenges.

Training Requirements

Training Overview

  • Duration: Multi-week course (specific hours vary by offering)
  • Cost: FREE - No cost for training
  • Format: Virtual (Zoom-based) or in-person options available
  • Capacity: Maximum of 50 applications accepted per training course
  • Curriculum: Knowledge and skills necessary for effective peer support

Training Content

Training builds upon participant experience with instruction in:

  • Foundational competencies consistent with SAMHSA national standards
  • Peer support principles and practices
  • Recovery-oriented approaches
  • Ethical standards and boundaries
  • Wellness and self-care
  • Advocacy and empowerment
  • Cultural competency

Competitive Selection Process

Applications are ranked on a points basis:

  • Previous peer-related training
  • Previous peer-related job experience
  • Quality of essay responses
  • Demonstrated desire to work as certified peer specialist
  • Understanding of mental health and substance use recovery principles
  • Support for many pathways to wellness

Facilitators virtually interview up to 30 applicants. Apply early when applications open!

Recertification Requirements

Recertification Cycle

Every Two Years from the year you passed the exam or last recertified

Recertification Deadline

September 30th (every two years from certification date)

Continuing Education Requirements

20 Hours of Professional Development

  • Must be completed during the two-year certification period
  • Must be relevant to peer support practice
  • Documentation required for recertification

Grace Period (1 month after deadline) $55.00 (no penalty)

Recertification Process

  1. Track 20 hours of professional development throughout certification period
  2. Submit recertification application by September 30th
  3. Submit documentation of continuing education
  4. Pay recertification fee ($55)

Scope of Practice

What Certified Peer Specialists Do

According to Wisconsin statute (DHS 75.03(22)), a certified peer specialist:

  • Has lived experience of mental illness or substance use disorders, or both
  • Completed formal training in peer specialist model
  • Holds department certification
  • Provides peer support based on lived experience
  • Works in mental health or substance use disorder support settings

What Certified Peer Specialists Are NOT

  • Not clinical staff - Cannot provide clinical services
  • Not therapists - Do not provide therapy
  • Not case managers - Do not provide case management (unless separately qualified)
  • Not medical professionals - Do not provide medical advice

Practice Settings

Certified Peer Specialists work in:

  • Community mental health centers
  • Substance use disorder treatment programs
  • Peer-run organizations
  • Recovery community centers
  • Hospitals and healthcare settings
  • Crisis programs
  • Supported housing programs
  • Correctional facilities (specialized training)

Professional Practice Guidelines for Wisconsin

Regulation Level: Wisconsin offers voluntary certifications for recovery professionals. Following ethical guidelines is essential even when not legally required.

Important: These guidelines apply to Recovery Coaches, Life Coaches, Interventionists, and Case Managers practicing in Wisconsin.

Recovery Coaches & Peer Support Specialists

✓ DO:

  • Ensure all clients have concurrent licensed clinical support
  • Maintain clear boundaries between peer support and therapy
  • Use coaching/peer support language, not clinical terminology
  • Obtain professional liability insurance
  • Provide written coaching agreements defining scope
  • Make referrals when clients need clinical intervention
  • Pursue relevant certifications (CRSS, CPS, CPRS)
  • Share lived experience appropriately and helpfully
  • Maintain continuing education in recovery support
  • Build referral network with licensed professionals

✗ DON'T:

  • Diagnose mental health or substance use disorders
  • Provide therapy or clinical treatment
  • Replace clinical care with coaching alone
  • Work with actively using clients without medical oversight
  • Use language that implies you're providing therapy
  • Practice without professional liability insurance
  • Work outside your scope of competence
  • Handle psychiatric crises without referring to professionals
  • Misrepresent your credentials or training
  • Engage in dual relationships with clients

Life Coaches Working with Recovery Clients

✓ DO:

  • Require clients to have therapist/addiction counselor
  • Focus on goal-setting and forward-looking coaching
  • Stay in life coaching scope (career, relationships, wellness)
  • Make immediate referrals for clinical issues
  • Use clear coaching contracts specifying you're not providing therapy
  • Maintain professional liability insurance
  • Build strong referral network of licensed professionals
  • Understand the line between coaching and counseling
  • Document referrals and scope boundaries
  • Consider ICF or other coaching certification

✗ DON'T:

  • Coach clients in active addiction without clinical support
  • Address trauma or clinical issues in coaching sessions
  • Diagnose or treat mental health conditions
  • Position coaching as alternative to therapy
  • Work with clients in psychiatric crisis
  • Use therapeutic techniques requiring clinical license
  • Coach clients who refuse to see licensed professionals
  • Guarantee recovery outcomes
  • Practice addiction counseling without license
  • Cross boundaries into clinical territory

Professional Interventionists

✓ DO:

  • Pursue professional certification (CIP, BRI, CAI)
  • Complete comprehensive intervention training (100+ hours)
  • Obtain professional liability insurance (essential)
  • Use evidence-based intervention models
  • Provide clear service agreements to families
  • Maintain referral relationships with treatment centers
  • Practice family-centered, compassionate intervention
  • Stay current on intervention best practices
  • Document all intervention services thoroughly
  • Follow ethical guidelines and professional standards

✗ DON'T:

  • Diagnose substance use or mental health disorders
  • Provide clinical treatment or therapy
  • Practice without professional liability insurance
  • Use coercive or overly confrontational tactics
  • Guarantee intervention outcomes to families
  • Work without proper training in intervention models
  • Misrepresent your credentials or success rates
  • Engage in unethical referral arrangements
  • Practice if you have active addiction issues
  • Cross professional boundaries with clients

Case Managers

✓ DO:

  • Obtain underlying professional license (RN, LCSW, LPC, etc.)
  • Pursue CCM or equivalent certification
  • Maintain professional liability insurance
  • Work within your professional scope of practice
  • Coordinate care across all providers effectively
  • Document all case management activities thoroughly
  • Follow HIPAA and confidentiality requirements
  • Advocate appropriately for client needs
  • Complete continuing education requirements
  • Join professional associations (CMSA, NASW, etc.)

✗ DON'T:

  • Practice without appropriate underlying license
  • Provide clinical services outside your scope
  • Diagnose conditions without proper credentials
  • Bill for services without proper certification
  • Practice across state lines without proper licensure
  • Violate client confidentiality or HIPAA
  • Accept inappropriate incentives from providers
  • Misrepresent credentials or certifications
  • Guarantee specific outcomes to clients
  • Engage in dual relationships with clients
